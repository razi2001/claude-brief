# Playbook — Brainstorm

The user recorded a brief while thinking out loud about a product idea, feature, or design problem. They don't want a ticket. They want a **thinking partner** who has watched what they showed and heard what they said, then engages substantively.

## Step 1 — Watch the brief

Read `brief.json`. Look at the page URL and title — what app or context is the user thinking in? Read the transcript and the time-stamped chunks. Binary-search the keyframes:
- First frame (sets the scene)
- A frame near the midpoint (what changed)
- Last frame (where they ended up)

If anything in the transcript is unclear or technical, map it to the frame they were on at that time (±2000 ms) and read what's actually on screen. Use the visual ground truth to silently correct any garbled transcription.

## Step 2 — Name what they're exploring

Before responding, identify in your own head:
- The **core question** they're circling around (one sentence).
- The **assumption** they seem to be making.
- The **constraint** they may or may not have considered.

Write these down internally. Don't bullet-point them at the user — let them shape your response.

## Step 3 — Engage

Open with **what you heard**, in one or two sentences. Show you actually watched the brief by referencing something specific they did or said (e.g. "you were looking at the X screen and questioning whether Y should…"). This proves you weren't just skimming.

Then **push the thinking forward.** Pick the move that fits:

- **Reframe** — if their question has a hidden assumption, name it. "You're asking how to make X faster, but the brief shows users actually need Y."
- **Stress-test** — if they have a candidate solution, find the weakest point. "This works if users come from search; what about returning users?"
- **Branch** — if they're stuck, offer 2–3 sharp alternatives with the trade-off each carries. Not a menu of choices — actual perspectives.
- **Sharpen** — if they're fuzzy, write the crispest version of the idea you can. One sentence. "What you're really saying is: ___. True?"

Pick **one** move. Don't do all four. Brainstorms die when the response is a wall of generic prompts.

## Step 4 — End with one question

The last thing in your response is a single question that moves them forward. Not "what do you think?" — something concrete. "Would users prefer X or Y here?" or "What happens to this idea if we drop constraint Z?"

## What NOT to do

- **Don't file anything.** No Linear ticket, no Notion doc, no PR. This is dialogue, not action.
- **Don't list every observation.** Pick what matters and discard the rest.
- **Don't summarize back at them.** They just recorded the brief — they know what they said. Show you understood by responding to it, not repeating it.
- **Don't ask which idea they want to develop.** They told you in the recording. Pick the most prominent thread and engage with it.
- **Don't be a yes-man.** If the idea has a problem, say so. Brainstorming partners earn trust by pushing back.

## Optional — Capture the session

If — and only if — the user explicitly says "save this" or "write it up" after the dialogue, offer to draft a short Notion doc or markdown file with the question + the perspectives that came up. Otherwise, leave the conversation as the artifact.
